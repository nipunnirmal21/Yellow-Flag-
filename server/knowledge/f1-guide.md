# Formula 1 basics

Formula 1 (F1) is the highest class of international single-seater open-wheel racing, run by the FIA. A season is a world championship: drivers score points at every Grand Prix, fighting for two titles — the World Drivers' Championship (WDC) and the World Constructors' Championship (WCC) for teams.

## Race weekend format

A normal weekend has three practice sessions (FP1, FP2, FP3), then Qualifying, then the Grand Prix on Sunday. Qualifying is a knockout: Q1 eliminates the slowest five, Q2 the next five, and Q3 decides pole position among the top ten. On sprint weekends the order changes: one practice session, then Sprint Qualifying (SQ1–SQ3), a short Sprint race of about 100 km, then normal Qualifying and the Grand Prix.

## Points system

Grand Prix points for the top 10: 25, 18, 15, 12, 10, 8, 6, 4, 2, 1. Sprint points for the top 8: 8, 7, 6, 5, 4, 3, 2, 1. Since 2025 there is no bonus point for fastest lap. Drivers' and constructors' championships both count these points; a team scores with both its cars.

## Flag meanings

- Yellow flag: danger ahead — slow down, no overtaking. Double yellow means be ready to stop. (This is where the channel name "Yellow Flag" comes from!)
- Green flag: track clear, racing resumes.
- Red flag: session stopped.
- Blue flag: a faster car is lapping you — let it through.
- Black flag: driver disqualified.
- Chequered flag: end of the session or race.

## The 2026 regulations — a new era

The 2026 season starts a major new rules era:

- 11 teams and 22 cars on the grid, with Cadillac joining as a brand-new team and Audi entering by taking over Sauber.
- New power units: roughly a 50/50 split between combustion engine and electric power, no MGU-H, and fully sustainable fuel.
- Smaller, lighter, more agile cars with active aerodynamics: X-mode for low drag on straights and Z-mode for downforce in corners.
- Manual Override mode gives a temporary electric power boost for overtaking, replacing the old DRS-style overtaking aid.

## All-time greats (quick facts)

Lewis Hamilton and Michael Schumacher share the record of 7 drivers' titles each. Ferrari is the most successful constructor in F1 history and the only team to have raced in every season since 1950.
